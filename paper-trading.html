Paper Trading: Practice Without Losing a Dime

Beginner · 6 min read

Airlines don't hand new pilots a 737 full of passengers and say "figure it out." They put them in a simulator, where a crash costs nothing but pride. Trading has the exact same thing — it's called paper trading, and this blog is literally named after it.

Paper trading means trading real markets, at real live prices, with fake money. The name comes from the old days when practice trades were scribbled on paper instead of phoned to a broker. Today it's built into almost every trading app: flip a switch, get $100,000 of pretend money, and every button works exactly like the real thing.

Why bother if the money's fake?

Because your first fifty trades are going to include some ugly ones. That's not pessimism, it's how learning works — nobody's first fifty free throws all go in either. Paper trading lets those inevitable rookie mistakes cost you nothing. You'll fat-finger an order and buy 100 shares instead of 10. You'll panic-sell at the worst moment. You'll hold a loser hoping it "comes back." Better to get those mistakes out of your system on a simulator.

How to start today, for free

Nearly every major broker has a paper mode: Webull and thinkorswim (Schwab) have excellent built-in simulators, TradingView has one attached to its charts, and Investopedia's Stock Simulator runs right in a browser with no download. Pick one, and here's the important part: set your fake balance to what you'd actually start with. If you'd realistically trade with $1,000, don't practice with the default $100,000 — practicing decisions you'll never face teaches you nothing.

Treat it like it's real. The only way paper trading fails is if you treat it like a video game. Follow real rules: plan every trade before you enter it, write down why, and review your trades weekly. Fake money, real discipline.

The one thing paper trading can't teach you

Honesty time: paper trading can't simulate fear. Losing $200 of pretend money feels like nothing; losing $200 of rent money makes your hands shake. That gap is real, and it's why some people paper trade perfectly and then fall apart with real money. The fix isn't to skip the simulator — it's to make the jump gradual: paper trade first, then start real trading with amounts so small the losses genuinely don't scare you, and scale up only as your results earn it.

How do you know you're ready for real money?

Not after a lucky week. A reasonable bar: at least one to three months of paper trading, at least 30–50 trades logged in your journal, a written plan you actually followed on most of them, and results that are at least break-even. If you can't be profitable with fake money and no pressure, real money and real pressure will not improve things.

The market isn't going anywhere. It'll still be there in three months — and you'll be walking in with a trained eye instead of a hopeful guess.
