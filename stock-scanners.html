Stock Scanners: How Traders Find What to Trade

Beginner · 6 min read

There are roughly 6,000 stocks trading on U.S. exchanges. Nobody — not you, not a Wall Street pro — can watch them all. So how does a trader decide what to look at each morning? They don't scroll through 6,000 charts. They use a scanner.

A stock scanner (also called a screener) is a search engine for stocks. Instead of typing "best pizza near me," you type conditions — show me stocks up more than 5% today, trading over a million shares, priced between $5 and $50 — and it instantly returns the short list that matches. Six thousand stocks become six. That's the whole job: turning an ocean into a fishing pond.

Why traders live by them

Most stocks do nothing interesting on most days — they drift on low volume, and trading them is like surfing on a lake. Traders make money on movement, and movement clusters in a handful of names each day: the ones with news, earnings, or unusual volume. The scanner's job is to find where the action is today, so you're not forcing trades on dead charts. As the saying goes: don't trade the stock you're married to, trade the stock that's moving.

What traders actually scan for

The classic beginner-friendly filters, and why each one exists: % gainers/losers (biggest movers of the day — movement is opportunity), relative volume (a stock trading 5× its normal volume means something is happening — this is many traders' favorite single filter), price range (e.g. $2–$100; extremely cheap stocks are often manipulated, extremely expensive ones need a big account), news catalyst (a mover with a reason tends to keep moving; a mover with no reason tends to snap back), and float (how many shares actually trade — fewer shares means faster, wilder moves, for better and worse).

A scanner finds candidates, not trades. This is the mistake to avoid: seeing a stock on a scanner and buying it because it's "up a lot." The scanner answers "what should I look at?" Your plan — entry, stop, target — answers "should I trade it?" Never let the first question replace the second.

A simple starter scan

If you want something to try in your paper trading account tomorrow morning, this classic finds "stocks in play": price between $2 and $50, up 4% or more on the day, volume already above 500,000 shares, with some news in the last day. Run it around market open, pick the two or three cleanest charts from the results, and watch how they behave. Don't trade them at first — just watch. Learning what "in play" looks like is a skill in itself.

The routine that makes scanners useful

Scanners reward consistency, not cleverness. The traders who benefit run the same scan at the same time every day, keep a short watchlist from the results, and journal which setups from the scan actually worked. After a month you'll know which scan results fit your strategy — and that's worth more than any premium subscription.
